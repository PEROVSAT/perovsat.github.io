# World Magnetic Model (WMM)

## Overview

The **World Magnetic Model (WMM)** module provides the Earth's magnetic field at the spacecraft's current position throughout the simulation. It serves as the environmental model for the Passive Magnetic Attitude Control (PMAC) system by supplying the magnetic field required to compute magnetic torques generated by the permanent magnet and hysteresis rods.

This project uses a **continuous implementation** of the World Magnetic Model, allowing both the magnetic field vector and its time derivative to be computed analytically. This improves numerical stability and provides smooth magnetic field data for the hysteresis rod model.

---

## Purpose

The WMM module is responsible for:

- Computing the Earth's magnetic field at the spacecraft position.
- Updating the magnetic field continuously as the spacecraft moves along its orbit.
- Computing the magnetic field rate of change.
- Publishing the magnetic field information for use by the PMAC modules.

---

## Background

Earth's magnetic field varies with latitude, longitude, altitude, and time. The World Magnetic Model represents this field using spherical harmonic coefficients published by the National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI) and the British Geological Survey (BGS).

The magnetic field is obtained from the scalar magnetic potential

$$
\mathbf{B} = -\nabla V
$$

where \(V\) is represented as a spherical harmonic expansion.

As the spacecraft travels through Low Earth Orbit, it experiences a continuously changing magnetic field, which drives the passive magnetic attitude control system.

---

## Continuous Magnetic Field Computation

The standard Basilisk WMM updates the magnetic field once every simulation step, producing a Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) approximation.

This project replaces the discrete implementation with a continuous formulation that computes both the magnetic field and its derivative analytically. This eliminates discontinuities that can occur when differentiating a sampled magnetic field.

---

## Magnetic Field Rate

The magnetic field experienced by the spacecraft changes according to

$$
\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{dt}
=
\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}
+
(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{B}
$$

where

- $\mathbf{v}$ is the spacecraft velocity.
- $\nabla\mathbf{B}$ is the spatial gradient of the magnetic field.

Since the Earth's secular magnetic variation is negligible over a single simulation,

$$
\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\approx0
$$

which simplifies the expression to

$$
\dot{\mathbf{B}}
=
(\nabla\mathbf{B})\mathbf{v}
$$

This analytical computation produces a smooth magnetic field derivative that is particularly useful for nonlinear hysteresis rod simulations.

---

## Inputs

The module requires the following inputs:

| Input | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Spacecraft Position | Current spacecraft position in the inertial frame |
| Spacecraft Velocity | Used to compute the magnetic field derivative |
| Simulation Epoch | Decimal year used by the WMM |
| WMM Coefficient File | Official Gauss coefficients describing Earth's magnetic field |

---

## Outputs

The module publishes the following outputs.

### Magnetic Field

$$
\mathbf{B}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
B_x \\
B_y \\
B_z
\end{bmatrix}
\quad [T]
$$

### Magnetic Field Rate

$$
\dot{\mathbf{B}}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
\dot{B}_x \\
\dot{B}_y \\
\dot{B}_z
\end{bmatrix}
\quad [T/s]
$$

These outputs are consumed by the Permanent Magnet and Hysteresis Rod modules to compute passive magnetic control torques.

## Advantages

Compared to the standard discrete implementation, the continuous WMM provides:

- Continuous magnetic field evaluation.
- Analytical magnetic field derivatives.
- Improved numerical stability.
- Better compatibility with nonlinear hysteresis models.
- Reduced numerical noise during long-duration simulations.

---

## Summary

The World Magnetic Model is the primary environmental model used by the PMAC simulation. It continuously computes Earth's magnetic field and its rate of change at the spacecraft's location, providing the magnetic inputs required for the Permanent Magnet and Hysteresis Rod modules to simulate passive magnetic attitude stabilization.
